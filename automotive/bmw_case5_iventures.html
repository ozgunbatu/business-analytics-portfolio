Investment Thesis & Decision Framework

Core Investment Themes (2024-2028)

Theme 1: Battery Ecosystem (30% allocation)

Thesis: Battery costs represent 40% of EV bill-of-materialsâ€”BMW must secure supply chain resilience while reducing per-kWh costs from â‚¬120 (2024) to <â‚¬60 (2030). Vertical integration via investments in raw materials (Lilac), manufacturing (Solid Power), and recycling (Redwood) de-risks supply shocks.

Target companies: Lithium extraction tech, solid-state batteries, battery management systems, recycling/circular economy

Strategic value: Joint development agreements, offtake guarantees, technology licensing
Theme 2: Software-Defined Vehicle (25% allocation)

Thesis: Vehicle value shifting from hardware (engine) to software (autonomous features, OTA updates, digital services). Tesla's software margin: 80%+ vs. BMW's hardware margin: 15%. Investments in AI infrastructure (Graphcore), edge computing, and cloud platforms enable BMW's SDV transformation.

Target companies: AI chips, ADAS software, OTA platforms, cybersecurity, data monetization

Strategic value: Technology stack integration, talent pipeline, product roadmap acceleration
Theme 3: Manufacturing 4.0 (20% allocation)

Thesis: Legacy automotive manufacturing designed for 100-year-old ICE processes. EV production requires 50% less labor, 70% fewer parts, radically different supply chain. Investments in robotics, AI quality control, and digital twins improve efficiency while reducing â‚¬5B annual manufacturing costs.

Target companies: Industrial robotics, computer vision QC, digital twins, autonomous material handling

Strategic value: Pilot deployments in BMW plants, cost reduction proof points
Theme 4: Circular Economy & Climate Tech (15% allocation)

Thesis: EU regulations mandate 55% emissions reduction by 2030â€”BMW's Scope 3 emissions (supply chain) represent 85% of total footprint. Investments in green materials, carbon capture, and remanufacturing create compliance pathway while differentiating brand.

Target companies: Sustainable materials, carbon accounting software, remanufacturing platforms

Strategic value: ESG reporting, regulatory compliance, premium brand positioning
Theme 5: Mobility Services (10% allocation)

Thesis: Car ownership declining in urban centersâ€”65% of Gen Z prefer access over ownership. BMW must hedge with mobility-as-a-service (MaaS) bets. Investments in car-sharing (ReachNow), ride-hailing platforms, and multimodal transport apps capture revenue from non-ownership models.

Target companies: MaaS platforms, micromobility, parking tech, multimodal apps

Strategic value: Business model optionality, customer data, urban partnership access

Lessons Learned & Evolution

Key Learning 1: Speed Mismatch
Corporate decision-making (6-month pilot approvals) misaligned with startup velocity (90-day iteration cycles). Solution: Created "Fast Track" programâ€”BMW i Ventures portfolio companies get pre-approved pilot framework, reducing time-to-test from 6 months to 6 weeks.
Key Learning 2: Cannibalization Fear
Internal BMW divisions resist innovations that threaten existing P&Ls (e.g., car-sharing cannibalizes vehicle sales). Solution: Ring-fenced i Ventures governanceâ€”investments approved independently, strategic integration negotiated post-validation.
Key Learning 3: Portfolio Construction
Early funds over-concentrated in mobility services (10 ride-sharing bets, 8 failed). Shifted to infrastructure and enabling tech with multiple use cases. Current portfolio: 60% B2B enablers, 40% B2C mobility services.
Benchmark Comparison: BMW i Ventures' 4.2x Fund I MOIC compares favorably to corporate VC median (2.1x) per CB Insights, though trails top-quartile traditional VCs (6.5x). Outperformance drivers: (1) Strong deal flow from strategic mandate, (2) Value-add through pilots/commercial partnerships, (3) Disciplined exit discipline (vs. corporate VCs holding strategically).
