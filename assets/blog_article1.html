The EV Transition Paradox: Why Legacy OEMs Struggle

By Özgün Batuhan Dogan | February 2025 | 7 min read

BMW, Mercedes, and VW possess everything Tesla built from scratch: engineering expertise, manufacturing scale, supplier relationships, brand equity, and multi-billion euro budgets. Yet they consistently trail Tesla in EV profitability, software integration, and speed-to-market. Why?

The answer isn't technical capability—it's organizational architecture. Legacy automakers are trapped by the very structures that made them successful in the ICE era.

The Cannibalization Trap

Every EV sold by BMW cannibalizes a more profitable ICE vehicle. Traditional cars generate 15-18% operating margins; early EVs operate near breakeven. For executives measured on quarterly earnings, the rational choice is delaying the transition—not accelerating it.

Data: BMW 3 Series (ICE) margin: ~16% | BMW i4 (EV) margin: ~8% | Tesla Model 3 margin: ~18%
Source: Company filings, Bernstein Research estimates

Platform Proliferation vs. Platform Thinking

Tesla builds all vehicles on 2 platforms. VW Group operates 12+ platforms across brands. This isn't perfectionism—it's organizational fragmentation preventing the economies of scale that make EVs profitable.

  • Tesla approach: Unified skateboard architecture, shared components, rapid iteration
  • Legacy approach: Model-specific engineering, bespoke components, lengthy development cycles

Result: Tesla launches new models in 18-24 months. BMW's iX took 6+ years from concept to production.

The Software Integration Problem

Legacy OEMs treat software as purchased components (like seats). Tesla treats it as the core product. This architectural difference cascades into everything:

  • BMW sources infotainment from suppliers → fragmented stack → slow updates → poor UX
  • Tesla vertically integrates → unified OS → weekly improvements → NPS advantage

VW's Cariad software unit has consumed €5B+ and missed every deadline. Why? You can't hire 5,000 engineers and overcome 100 years of hardware-first culture overnight.

Dealer Network Misalignment

Franchised dealers resist EVs because they destroy service revenue (EVs need 70% less maintenance). Even where legally possible, antagonizing dealers who control customer relationships risks torpedoing ICE sales that still fund EV development.

Tesla's advantage: Direct sales = real-time demand signals, customer data ownership, pricing flexibility, service integration. Legacy OEMs can't replicate this in markets with dealer franchise protection laws.

What Would It Take to Win?

Three painful but necessary choices:

  1. Separate EV business units with P&L autonomy (like GM's BrightDrop)—ring-fence operations, accept short-term earnings volatility
  2. Platform consolidation (killing sacred cows)—one architecture across brands, no exceptions for Audi/Porsche
  3. Acquire speed through M&A—buy software/autonomy startups, don't integrate them into the mothership

The Uncomfortable Truth

Most legacy OEMs will survive—but as smaller, less profitable companies. VW's 40% market share becomes 25% when Chinese competitors (BYD) and software-first players command premium segments. BMW's 18% margins compress to 10% as software economics replace hardware economics.

For strategists entering this space: Legacy automotive's challenges aren't about capability—they're about institutional constraints. The opportunity lies in helping giants navigate transformation... or building the startups that make them irrelevant.
